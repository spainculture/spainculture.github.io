Nestled along the southeastern coast of Cuba, Manzanillo is a city that pulses with life, color, and a rich cultural heritage. Often overshadowed by Havana or Santiago de Cuba, this coastal town offers an authentic glimpse into the soul of Cuban life. From its Afro-Cuban roots to its resilient spirit in the face of global challenges, Manzanillo’s culture is a tapestry of history, music, and community.

The Heartbeat of Manzanillo: Music and Dance

Son Cubano and Changüí

Manzanillo is a cradle of traditional Cuban music, particularly son cubano and changüí. These genres, born from the fusion of African rhythms and Spanish melodies, are the lifeblood of local celebrations. Walk through the streets on any given evening, and you’ll hear the unmistakable sound of the tres (a Cuban guitar-like instrument) and the rhythmic clapping of clave.

In recent years, global interest in Afro-Cuban music has surged, with artists like Buena Vista Social Club bringing these sounds to international audiences. Manzanillo, however, remains a place where the music is lived, not just performed.

The Legacy of the Tumba Francesa

One of the most fascinating cultural expressions in Manzanillo is the Tumba Francesa, a dance and music tradition brought by Haitian slaves in the 18th century. Recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage, this art form blends West African drumming with French colonial dance styles. Today, local groups like La Pompadour keep this tradition alive, performing in vibrant costumes that tell stories of resistance and resilience.

Cuisine: A Taste of Manzanillo’s Soul

Seafood and the Caribbean Diet

Located by the sea, Manzanillo’s cuisine is heavily influenced by its maritime environment. Dishes like caldosa (a hearty seafood stew) and bacalao (salted cod) are staples. With global concerns about sustainable fishing, Manzanillo’s fishermen have become inadvertent ambassadors for small-scale, eco-friendly practices—a stark contrast to industrial fishing threatening marine ecosystems worldwide.

The Coffee Culture

Cuban coffee is legendary, and Manzanillo is no exception. The region’s cafeteros (coffee growers) have faced challenges due to climate change, with rising temperatures affecting yields. Yet, the tradition of the cafecito—a strong, sweet espresso—remains a daily ritual. In a world grappling with food insecurity, Manzanillo’s coffee culture is a reminder of how local traditions can adapt to global crises.

Challenges and Resilience: Manzanillo in a Changing World

Economic Sanctions and Local Ingenuity

Cuba’s economy has long been strained by U.S. sanctions, and Manzanillo is no exception. Yet, the town’s residents have turned to creative solutions. Inventar (to invent) is a way of life here—from repurposing old car parts to crafting artisanal goods. In an era of global supply chain disruptions, Manzanillo’s resourcefulness offers lessons in sustainability.

Climate Change and Coastal Vulnerability

As a coastal city, Manzanillo is on the front lines of climate change. Rising sea levels and stronger hurricanes threaten its infrastructure. Yet, the community’s deep connection to the land and sea fuels grassroots efforts to protect their home. Local initiatives, like mangrove restoration, highlight how traditional knowledge can combat modern environmental crises.

Festivals: Where Culture Comes Alive

Carnaval de Manzanillo

Every summer, the Carnaval de Manzanillo transforms the city into a whirlwind of color and sound. Unlike the more commercialized carnivals in Rio or New Orleans, this event is deeply communal. Floats adorned with political satire—a nod to Cuba’s tradition of social commentary—parade alongside comparsas (dance troupes) in dazzling costumes.

Fiesta del Fuego

Held annually, the Fiesta del Fuego (Festival of Fire) celebrates Afro-Caribbean culture. With drum circles, fire dances, and spiritual ceremonies, it’s a powerful reminder of Cuba’s African roots. In a world where racial justice movements are gaining momentum, this festival underscores the importance of cultural preservation.

Final Thoughts

Manzanillo may not be on every traveler’s radar, but its culture is a microcosm of Cuba’s soul. From its music and food to its resilience in the face of global challenges, this city is a testament to the power of community and tradition. As the world grapples with inequality, climate change, and cultural homogenization, places like Manzanillo remind us of the beauty and strength found in local identities.
